Seeing the Solar System

Here is the night sky as you can see it with your own eyes under perfect conditions - say on a good night at the summit of Cerro Tololo in Chile. It consists of a couple thousand stars, the Milky Way and a few nebulae (galaxies or gas clouds), plus the five planets you can see by naked eye. The Sun appears at its proper location, even though you can never see it among the stars. The one glaring omission is the Moon, which is not shown at all because it moves through the sky at least ten times faster than the Sun or planets, and because untangling the details of its motion turns out to be far more difficult than understanding the motion of the Sun and planets - the solar system proper.

What are you looking at? Can you prove it just from what you see here? Those are the questions you will explore here.

Except for the Sun and planets, nearly everything you see in the sky looks the same as it did to the original builders of Stonehenge. Those six bodies (and the Moon) are almost the only things you can see in the sky that move perceptably relative to the stars. (The occasional comet is the only notable exception.) The positions of the Sun and planets you see here are computed using formulas from JPL, which are exceedingly accurate from 5000 years in the past to 1000 years in the future.

Other caveats:

  • The planets and stars have no discernable size to your naked eye - they are mere points of light as far as anyone can see. The different sizes of the spots representing them here are a fiction to give you some impression of their relative brightnesses.
  • The disk of the Sun is actually about half the size shown here.
  • This simulation does not show the brightness changes of the planets, though the changes are easy to see for Venus and Mars.
  • You can't see anything closer than 15 or 20 degrees (about the size of Orion) from the Sun. (To accurately measure the position of the Sun relative to the stars, you need a good clock!)
  • The best possible naked eye measurements of the positions of the Sun and planets are accurate to about 1/30 degree, about 1/15 the diameter of the Sun or Moon. (Perfect human vision can barely discern two marks 1/60 degree apart.)
