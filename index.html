Seeing the Solar System

Here is the night sky as you can see it with your own eyes under perfect conditions - say on a good night at the summit of Cerro Tololo in Chile. Only the Moon is omitted, because it moves so fast compared to everything else you can see it would be just a blur, and because on the scale of the solar system you discover the Moon is part of the Earth.

What are you looking at? Can you prove it just from what you see in the sky? You will explore these questions here.

Nearly everything you see in the sky is unchanging - it looks the same now as it did to the original builders of Stonehenge. The Sun and planets (plus the Moon and, rarely, a comet) are the only things you can see in the sky that move perceptably relative to the stars. How can you work out the shapes and relative sizes of the orbits of the planets? Can different models describe what you see here, or is there only one possible explanation?

Here you can explore planetary motion with each passing year compressed into a bit less than ten seconds.

Caveats:

  • The solid blue line is the ecliptic plane; the dashed blue line is the equatorial plane. Solid and dashed crosses are the corresponding poles.
  • Unlike the dots that represent them here, the planets and stars have no discernable size to your naked eye - they are mere points of light as far as anyone can see.
  • The disk of the Sun is actually about half the size shown here.
  • Changes in brightness of the planets are not depicted.
  • You can't see anything closer than 15 or 20 degrees (about the size of Orion) from the Sun - in other words within about an hour of sunset or sunrise.

How the Sun and Planets Move

The Sun and planets move through the constellations of the Zodiac. The Sun moves exactly around a great circle in the sky called the ecliptic, which is the horizontal blue line. The planets execute a much more complicated motion, always remaining near the ecliptic. The planets usually move around the Zodiac in the same west-to-east direction as the Sun, but occasionally reverse and move "retrograde" through the Zodiac from east to west. (Note that when you look up at the sky instead of down at a map, east is to your left and west is to your right, when north is up!)

You will want to "drag the sky" with your mouse to look around - otherwise you may need to wait a long time for slow-moving Jupiter and Saturn to come into view. It's also fun to lok for your favorite constellations. Orion and the Big Dipper are easy to find. Cassiopeia and the Southern Cross are harder because they lie very near the dense star fields of the Milky Way.

Notice that the planets naturally divide in two groups: Mercury and Venus always stay near the Sun, while Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn can appear anywhere on the ecliptic relative to the Sun. Arranged by their greatest speed relative to the stars, from fastest to slowest the order is Mercury, Venus, the Sun, Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn. In what follows, we will consider only the Sun (obviously a special simpler case), Venus as a representative of the faster planets, and Mars as a representative of the slower planets.

Sun's Motion Not Quite Uniform

While the motion of the Sun is far simpler than the planets, it is not as simple as it could be. Although confined exactly to the ecliptic, the Sun speeds up and slows down slightly over the course of the year. If you count the days from the March equinox to the December equinox you find there are 186, but from the December equinox back to the March equinox takes only 179 days - even though the equinoxes are exactly 180 degrees apart on the ecliptic.

The only way you can see this tiny effect is to compare the actual position of the Sun to a point on the ecliptic called the "mean Sun" - an imaginary direction which moves around the ecliptic at an exactly uniform rate. The mean Sun is at the same point as the actual Sun at perihelion, when Earth is closest to the Sun in early January, and at aphelion, when Earth is farthest from the Sun in early July. We understand that the Earth moves around the Sun faster near perihelion, and slower near aphelion, but the concept of the mean Sun has been around since ancient times - long before its explanation in terms of the Earth's orbit was known.

Note that the blue ecliptic and celestial equator lines were also well-known in ancient times. Hence the equinoxes and the slight non-uniformity in the Sun's motion around the ecliptic were also known, probably since prehistoric times.

Notice the Sun reaches its maximum westward position relative to the mean Sun just before reaching the brightest part of the Milky way, and its maximum eastward position just before passing over Orion, year after year. Although the speed of the Sun varies, the pattern of those variations repeats exactly every year.

How Venus Moves

Venus oscillates back and forth from about 48 degrees west of the Sun to 48 degrees east of the Sun, completing one cycle roughly every 584 days. Unlike the motion of the Sun around the ecliptic, these cycles never repeat exactly - both the period and the path vary without any obvious pattern from one cycle to the next. When west of the Sun, Venus is the morning star, when east of the Sun it is the evening star. Its oscillations are not at all symmetrical: Venus slowly overtakes the Sun during its prograde motion passing from the morning to the evening side of the Sun, then quickly snaps back retrograde from the evening to the morning side.

Ancient Egyptian and Babylonian astronomers pointed out that this motion looks very much as if Venus is moving around the Sun in the same direction as the Sun is moving around the ecliptic. Our nearly edge-on view of this roughly circular motion makes Venus appear to oscillate from one side of the Sun to the other in this asymmetric way. This observation is undoubtedly the origin of the epicycle model of planetary motion worked out in detail by ancient Greek astronomers which held sway for nearly two millenia until Copernicus.

How Mars Moves

The motion of Mars is much harder to describe than the motion of Venus. The connection to the Sun is much subtler: Mars is always in the middle of its retrograde motion when it is exactly opposite the Sun. The anti-sun direction on the ecliptic is marked to help you notice this regularity. By turning to keep the Sun at the center of the view, you could watch Venus oscillate back and forth. If you want to watch Mars in a similar way, you need to turn to keep an imaginary direction in space, labeled "sun-mars" for reasons to be described later, at the center of your view: Mars oscillates back and forth around this sun-mars direction in much the way Venus oscillates around the Sun direction.

Once again, Mars slowly overtakes the sun-mars direction during the prograde part of its motion, then more quickly returns to the west side of the sun-mars direction in its retrograde part. The sun-mars direction is constructed to align with Mars itself in the middle of its prograde part exactly when the Sun overtakes it (called conjunction), and again in the middle of its retrograde part exactly when it crosses the anti-sun direction (called opposition). Be sure to watch for these moments when the Sun and anti-sun pass Mars. One whole cycle takes 780 days on average, but is considerably more irregular than the 584 day Venus cycle.

Although not depicted here, Mars is dramatically brighter near opposition than during the prograde part of its motion, a strong hint that it is much closer to us then.

Oppositions are extremely important times to make careful observations of Mars (and Jupiter and Saturn), because they can be measured very accurately. Conjuctions, when a planet passes either directly behind or (in the case of Venus and Mercury) directly in front of the Sun, would be just as important for understanding the motion, but can never be measured very accurately. Since you can never see anything in the sky anywhere near the Sun, you can only estimate the exact time of a conjunction. (Unless you have an essentially perfect theory of planetary motion!)

How to Model Planetary Motion

Most popular discussions of the history of solar system models concentrate on the shift from geocentric (Earth-centered) models to heliocentric (Sun-centered) models. But the real breakthrough was made by Kepler in 1609, and does not have much to do with whether you think the Sun or the Earth is at the center of the solar system. After all, the heliocentrism dates at least back to Aristarchus in the century before Hipparchus. Hipparchus, the greatest astronomer of antiquity, carefully studied the idea, and judged that the mathematically equivalent geocentric model provided a simpler description of planetary motion.

In fact, there is no way to define or establish which body, if any, in the solar system is at rest, no matter how carefully you measure the positions of planets. The sense in which the Sun is the center of the solar system is a theoretical prediction of Newtonian mechanics (published in 1687), which could not be observationally confirmed until centuries later (from stellar parallax).

Ultimately, all orbits are simply imaginary lines - there are no railroad tracks in space. The heliocentric orbits we all learn to draw in school are not the only reasonable choice for how to think about what you see in the sky. In many ways, the older geocentric picture provides a more immediate description of how the planets move, so it's worth explaining the relationship between the model we learned and the ancient models. If you want to master any subject, you need to be able to see it from as many perspectives as possible.

Incidentally, what Kepler really did - one of the greatest scientific advances of all time - was figure out how to survey the shapes of the orbits of Mars and of Earth (or the Sun). For millenia even the greatest astronomers had merely guessed shapes and compared their guesses to observations. Kepler was the first to actually measure the shapes of the orbits. Before we get to that, let's take a look at the various different ways people have imagined planetary orbits.

Venus's orbit viewed from Earth

Since Venus appears to oscillate from the morning to the evening side of the Sun, ancient astronomers in Egypt and Babylon imagined that we had a nearly edge on view of an orbit around the Sun. The asymmetry in the prograde and retrograde parts of this loop hints that Venus passes between the Earth and Sun on the retrograde part, and behind the Sun on the prograde part, moving arond the Sun in the same west-to-east direction as the Sun moves around the Zodiac.

When we draw our view from Earth of Venus's orbit around the Sun, notice that its projection on the sky changes shape during the course of the year. In fact, the orbit remains in a fixed orientation relative to the stars, which is slightly tilted from the ecliptic. The apparent changes in shape exactly repeat every year as the Earth returns to the same vantage point. For example, early in December when the Sun passes the brightest knot of the Milky Way, the Earth crosses the plane of Venus's orbit, and the orbit appears to collapse into a line. This crossing happens at the same time every year, so you can see that Venus's orbit is fixed relative to the stars. (This isn't quite true. If you go to a date several thousand years in the past, you will notice that Venus's orbit - and Earth's orbit too - do change very slowly.)

Venus's orbit viewed from deep space

Orbits are imaginary paths the planets follow, and you need to imagine them as three dimensional curves, not just their projections onto the sky. The best vantage point is far above the plane of the ecliptic, where you can see they are very nearly circles. From this vantage point, a second orbit becomes visible - the path the Sun follows around the Earth. Yes, we all learn that the Earth "really" goes around the Sun, but there isn't any geometrical reason we can't draw that orbit with the Earth at the center: Each point on the Sun's orbit lies in the direction we observe the Sun, at the distance of the Sun.

When you draw this orbit of the Sun around Earth, it doesn't change the fact that Venus orbits the Sun. Hence you very naturally arrive at a picture in which the center of Venus's orbit orbits around the Earth. The ancient Greeks called what we call Venus's "orbit" its "epicycle". The center of the epicycle follows a path they called the "deferent", but which we've described as the "Sun's orbit".

The picture you are looking at here is, qualitatively at least, the picture Hipparchus and the other great astronomers of ancient Greece used to think about how Venus moves. It is the picture in the Almagest, which was the starting point for the great Islamic astronomers of the middle ages as well.

Venus's heliocentric orbit

Here are the orbits of Venus and Earth the way we were all taught in school. On the plus side, we draw the two orbits with only a single center - the Sun. On the minus side, when we picture the Earth moving around the Sun, it's much harder to visualize how the Earth-Venus line is changing - but that direction is where we actually see Venus in the sky! From our point of view on Earth, Venus oscillates from one side of the Sun to the other as the Sun moves around the Zodiac - which is exactly what the epicycle picture of the two orbits shows. It's not hard to understand why the ancient astronomers might prefer this more direct description of what we actually see to the modest simplification of a single center.

You should check and uncheck the "sun center" box several times to be sure you understand the relationship between the heliocentric and geocentric pictures of the motion of Venus. The relative positions of the three objects - Venus, Earth, and Sun - are exactly the same in both models. The orbit of Venus is exactly the same. The orbit of the Sun in the geocentric picture is exactly the same shape as the orbit of Earth in the heliocentric picture, but rotated 180 degrees to swap the centers.
